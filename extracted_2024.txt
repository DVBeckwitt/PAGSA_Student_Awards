Eclipses
Our planet experiences two eclipse seasons annually when the Moon’s orbital path crosses directly between Earth and the Sun. Our location afforded us viewing and outreaching opportunities for both most recent eclipses, with a 60% partial view of last November’s (2023)annularsolareclipseandanalmost95%totality experience for the April 8, 2024, total eclipse.

Professor Haojing Yan was promoted to full professor in September 2024.He has continued leading his team using the data from the James Webb Space Telescope toexploretheearlyUniverse.
found a potential filament structure at redshift of 8.3 (corresponding to 610 million years after the Big Bang), signposted by four "infant" galaxies (less than 30 million years old) crunched into a small volume. These are the youngest (albeit not the earliest) galaxies ever reported in the literature; in other words, we are witnessing the structure in the making. The team has also identified a disk-shape galaxy at redshift of 5.289 (1.1 billion years after the Big Bang), a very early time that the previous theoretical models all said that disk- shape galaxies (i.e., systems dominated by rotation but not random motion) would not have come into being. This galaxy is the secondhighest-redshiftdiskgalaxyconfirmed todate,comingshortbyonly0.1inredshiftto the champion.

SpotlightonourScholarshipFunds
Thedepartmentisabletooffermanyscholarshipstoitsstudentsbecauseofthegenerosityofits alumni and faculty. These funds, established out of a love for physics and support of higher education, have benefited our students for many years. One fund, the Melvin Y. Mora Undergraduate Scholarship Fund, was established several years ago by students in the department who gave generously to honor one of their peers.

Fromfall 2005 newsletter:
Melvin Mora was killed in the line of duty just before his graduation date.Army Reserve Sergeant MelvinY. Mora, a Mizzou physics major killed in Iraq during an ambush in the line of dutyJune6,2004,wasasenioratMizzouandclosetograduatingwithhisbachelor’sdegree.To honor him, fellow undergraduate students organized a scholarship fund to help students who share Mora’s love of physics achieve their academic goals in his name. The MelvinY. Mora Scholarship Fund is an endowed fund that will provide a scholarship for a deserving physics major every year. Physics students worked on fund-raising projects during the 2004-05 school year and plan to continue to solicit funds during the 2005-06 academic year. The students are committed to seeing MelvinY. Mora’s name live on in the MU physics department, and they welcome contributions with gratitude.

Donorsinterestedincontributingtoanyofthefunds,ormakinganymonetarygifttothe department may contact Dr. Paul Miceli, Professor and Chair, at micelip@missouri.edu

In Memoriam



Former MU professor Joseph E. Willett of Columbia, Missouri passed away at the age of 94 on November 6, 2023. He was born inAlbany, Missouri on June 9, 1929, and completed his undergraduate and graduate work at the UniversityofMissouri-Columbia,receivinghisPh.D.in1956, working with Bernard Goodman in the area of quantum statistical mechanics.
After working in governmental and industrial research positionshereturnedtotheUniversityofMissouriin1965asa professor where he taught in the Department of Physics and Astronomy for over 40 years. His research in nuclear fusion
wasintentionallydesignedtohelpcarveapathforcleanenergy.Althoughhewasaphysicist by day and loved to teach graduate and undergraduate students, he was a jazz musician by night and organized his first band at the age of 13.Dr.Willett was a talented piano player, but tenor sax and clarinet were his passion. Well-
knowninthelocalmusicscene,heplayedinvenues all over Columbia for over 50 years and was considered to be one of the best clarinet players of his era. Dr. Willett retired from the University in 2004.
